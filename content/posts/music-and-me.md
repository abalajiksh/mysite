## Why

Most of what I write here is a record of how something was built. The wiring, the config files, the dead ends, the moment it finally worked. I like those posts. They are honest in a particular, mechanical way — you can follow the steps and arrive where I arrived.

This is not one of those.

This is the long version. And the strange thing about the long version is that it is not actually very long in calendar time. A seed was planted a couple of years ago, quietly, and then for over a year it just sat there doing nothing. The real thing — the obsession, the collection, the gear, the slow dismantling of everything I thought I knew about sound — happened *fast*, in a frantic burst across the back half of a single year, faster than I would like to admit. That is the part I want to tell you about. Not because the timeline is impressive, but because that is genuinely how it felt: years of nothing, and then a few months of falling.

It is about how I already loved music before any of this began. How I then learned to *hear* it, which turns out to be a separate and stranger skill entirely. How a single recording has held me together on nights when I was not sure anything would. And how, at the end of all of it, the physicist in me stopped being a passenger and started building the tools to fight back.

I want to tell it properly, which means slowly. There is a section near the end where I stop hiding behind cables and DACs and signal paths and just say the true thing plainly. If you came for gear comparisons, you will find some on the way, and you are welcome to leave before the floor drops out. I will not hold it against you.

The rest of you — settle in.

## The Listener Before the Hearing

I need you to understand that I did not arrive at music through audio equipment. I arrived at audio equipment through music. The order matters, and most of the people in this hobby get it backwards.

From January of 2023 onward, I lived on Spotify, and I lived on it *well*. By the time I left I had amassed something past two thousand liked songs, and they were not a monoculture — English, French, German, Tamil, Malayalam, Hindi, Korean, whatever had found me and refused to leave. I followed sound across languages I did not speak the way you follow a smell through a house: the French and the German and the Korean reached me through melody and texture and the sheer physical shape of a voice, long before I understood a single word, and most of the time I never bothered to translate, because the body had already decided. The Tamil I understood completely, because it is mine. More on that later. It costs me something to write it.

But here is the part that matters for everything that comes after: I was never the listener the streaming services are built for. I never lived on the algorithmic playlists, the Billboard-shaped slurry, the *Discover Weekly* conveyor belt. I hunted by *artist*. I would find someone, and then I would go through their work, song by song, record by record, the way you read an author rather than a sentence. The machine was always trying to hand me a mood and a vibe and a lukewarm forty-song queue, and I always walked past it to go find a person who made something on purpose.

I loved music. Two thousand songs and six languages of loving it. What I had not yet learned — what I did not even know there was *to* learn — was how to hear it.

## The Spark

The seed, when it came, did not come from music at all. It came from a movie, in the dark, and it is embarrassing, so I will tell it exactly as it happened.

It was a theater, dark in the particular way only theaters are dark, and the film was *Dune: Part Two*. This was a couple of years ago now. I had no special expectations. I went the way anyone goes — for the spectacle, the sandworms, Villeneuve doing his patient, monolithic thing with the camera. And for a while that is what I got, and it was wonderful.

And then the low end arrived.

I want to be careful with the word *arrived*, because I mean it. The bass in that film did not play. It did not come out of speakers and travel to my ears in the ordinary way sound is supposed to. It appeared, fully formed, *inside my chest*, as if the room had decided to breathe and I happened to be standing in its lungs. Zimmer's score and the sound design beneath it had been mastered by people who understood that some sounds are not meant to be heard so much as *felt*, and the theater had the system to deliver it, and for two and a half hours I sat in the dark and let something enormous move through me.

I went back. I told myself it was for the film. It was not for the film. I went back a third time, and by then I had stopped lying to myself about why. I walked out into the cold with a single, crystalline, slightly unhinged thought: *I have to have this at home.*

So I bought a soundbar. And then I watched the films again — not for Villeneuve, not for the story I now knew by heart, but for the *sound*.

And then — and this is the part the audiophile origin stories always leave out, because it ruins the clean mythology — nothing happened. For more than a year, nothing happened. I did not become an audio nutcase. I had a nicer movie setup and I went back to my life: two thousand Spotify songs, the occasional film revisited for its music. *The Breakfast Club* still comes around on my shelf about every two months, because a great soundtrack is a thing I will never stop returning to, and I want to be clear that I never abandoned movies for music — they have always lived side by side. The seed from that dark theater just sat there, dormant, for a year and change, waiting. I had no idea it was a seed at all. You never do.

## The Plunge

Then, in the late summer of 2025, the seed went off all at once, and the trigger was a friend with an iPod.

Up to that point my portable life had sounded *fine*, by which I mean I had no idea what it was missing. I listened on a pair of Sennheiser Momentum True Wireless 4 buds, over Bluetooth, on whatever lossy Dolby-flavoured profile the phone reached for by default, and it was pleasant and convenient and wireless — the audio equivalent of a meal that fills you up and leaves no memory of itself.

Then a friend handed me his rig: a Truthear Zero plugged into an iPod Classic, sixth generation, playing music in ALAC. Wired. Lossless. Old hardware doing exactly one thing without compromise. And it stopped me. I do not have a more dignified way to put it. Something in the texture of it — the space, the *unsquashedness* of it — landed somewhere the Bluetooth buds had never once reached, and I could not un-feel it.

Then he insisted I put the Zero on my *own* phone — my Xperia 1 V, the thing I carried every day — just to hear it on my hardware, with my Bluetooth buds sitting right there for the comparison. And even on my phone, even streaming, the cheap wired thing buried my Sennheisers. We sat there poking at the phone's audio settings like two idiots who had found a loose thread, and that is how we stumbled onto DSEE Ultimate — Sony's upscaler, quietly trying to reconstruct what the stream had thrown away — and it made the whole thing sound *marvelous*. That was the moment the question lodged itself and refused to leave: if a borrowed earphone and a settings toggle could do *this*, what on earth had I been missing all this time, and what would the *real* thing sound like — the actual lossless file, wired, untouched, with nothing standing in the way? Within weeks that single question had burned clean through my entire relationship with how I listened.

I cancelled my student Pro plan in September. After two and a half years and two thousand liked songs, I just turned it off. And in its place I started to build something that was *mine*.

I already had a Plex server — it had been running for years, quietly serving up my movie collection, and it had genuinely never once occurred to me to put music on it. Now I started feeding it music, and I could not stop. My own CDs first, the small sensible stack a person accumulates and forgets. Then my family's collection, boxes of discs that had not seen a laser in a decade, fed one by one into a drive and resolved into FLAC — lossless, permanent, *mine*. And when that ran dry, I started buying. Qobuz[^1] for the catalogue and the convenience. Bandcamp[^2] for something better — because on Bandcamp, more often than not, the artist hands you their actual master, the file as it left their hands, not a lossy render squeezed down for a phone speaker on a train.

The library did not grow. It *detonated*. Between the ripping and a buying spree I am slightly afraid to tally, it is north of four and a half terabytes of FLAC now. I am not going to defend the size of it. I am only reporting it, the way you report the weather.

A collection that size is not a collection anymore; it is a *problem*, and problems are solved with tools. The one I reach for most is a little program I wrote called `mithrandir`[^8]. An album image arrives as one long unbroken file with a cue sheet describing where the tracks live inside it, and someone has to carve that monolith into songs and tag each one correctly so the whole thing does not descend into chaos. `mithrandir` does the carving. I named it after Gandalf, and I stand by it. He also spent a great deal of his time making sure things ended up exactly where they were supposed to be, usually while everyone around him was making it harder.

## Learning to Hear

Here is something no one tells you when you start, because it sounds insufferable, and it is, but it is also true: **you cannot hear a difference you have not been trained to hear.**

The ear is not a passive instrument. It is a skill, built like any other, and at the beginning you are nearly deaf to everything that will later seem obvious. The differences are *there* — they were always there — but you walk past them the way you walk past a language you do not speak, hearing only undifferentiated noise where someone else hears meaning.

My training was quick, and it was not the smooth ascending ladder these stories like to pretend to be. It was a series of deliberate experiments, and I ran them the way I run everything — with research, a hypothesis, and an embarrassing amount of confidence in both.

The very first thing I bought was almost a joke: a cheap in-ear monitor off Amazon that cost me thirteen euros. Thirteen. And I could not believe my ears. That something costing less than a lunch could crack music open like that told me everything about how poorly convenience had been serving me — and it was that thirteen-euro punt, not any expensive epiphany, that actually committed me, because now I *had* to know how far down the rabbit hole went.

So I did what I do. I went and read the IEM subreddit's price-tier ranking list like scripture, and I made my next choice on a theory: I would go for a *warm-neutral* tuning, because — and I wince writing this now — I genuinely believed that *neutral is neutral*. As though there were one true flat reference and everything else were merely a deviation from it. There was a second, less defensible reason, too: the Truthear Hexa is *boxy* — square-shouldered, blunt, honest-looking — and I liked that far more than the weird organic blobs most in-ears are shaped like. So I bought the Hexa, warm-neutral and good-looking both, the careful and faintly vain pick of a man who thought neutrality was a settled question.

It is not a settled question. Tuning is not a ladder with accuracy at the top; it is a *space* of choices, and "neutral" is just one opinionated point inside it. I only learned that by running the opposite experiment. Later, on purpose, I bought the Letshuoer S08 to find out what all the fuss about *V-shaped* tuning was — the scooped mids, the lifted bass and treble, the smile-curve that is supposed to make everything sound exciting. And I did not like it. Not a little. But the thing that actually mattered was not the dislike; it was that I could now say precisely *why* — could point straight at the recessed mids, at the way the voices had been shoved a step back to make room for a sparkle I had not asked for. That was the real result of the experiment. Not the earphone. The fact that I had opinions now, and reasons sitting underneath them.

Then, over the ears, the Sennheiser HD 569 — and with each of these, almost imperceptibly, the deafness lifted. A treble I would once have called *fine* revealed itself as smeared and glassy. A soundstage I would once have called *wide* collapsed, under scrutiny, into a flat line painted between my ears. I started to hear the *room* a recording was made in. The decay of a note after the finger leaves the string. The space between instruments.

And here is the cruel part, the part that makes this a one-way door: once you can hear it, you can never un-hear it. There is no path back to the blissful, undiscerning ear you started with. That version of you is gone, locked on the other side of a door that only opens outward. You have traded contentment for perception, and nobody asks whether you wanted to make the trade, because by the time you understand it was a trade, it is already done.

I would not go back. But there is a small grief in it, too, and I want to be honest about that.

And there is a consolation folded into the grief, which took me longer to find: the skill, once it is built, is *yours*. It stops depending on the equipment that taught it. I could sell every box in this room tomorrow and still hear exactly what a crushed master is doing through a cheap pair of earphones, because the hearing was never in the gear — it was only ever trained *by* it. It is the same thing that happens on a bike. You learn balance and line and weight on a sharp, unforgiving road bike that punishes every lazy input, and once your body knows it, it knows it; put yourself on some heavy rental afterward and the instinct rides the lesser machine just fine. The capable thing raises your ceiling. The ceiling, once raised, stops caring about the floor.

## The Overhaul

And then came the reckoning. The build I still listen on today.

A Schiit Mimir, feeding a Midgaard headphone amplifier through a pair of balanced XLR cables, driving the Sennheiser HD 650 — open-backed, honest, unforgiving. A Sony Walkman for the hours I am away from the desk. I could describe the sound, but other people have done that better, and the sound is not actually the part I am proudest of.

The part I am proudest of is *invisible*.

On my Fedora Sway desktop, I reached into the audio stack and pulled the Mimir out of PipeWire entirely. I gave it direct ALSA access — no resampling, no mixing, no helpful little sample-rate conversion happening somewhere in the dark where I could not see it. And I want to explain why, because the reason is the whole of me in one decision.

I am a theoretical physicist. The instinct that governs everything I do is this: *if you want to know what something actually is, you control every variable in the path you possibly can.* Every stage that touches a thing on its way to you is a stage that introduces something — a rounding, an assumption, a "helpful" adjustment — that you did not ask for and cannot fully account for. And the trouble with the things you cannot account for is that you can never again be sure what you are hearing is the signal and what is the apparatus. So you strip the apparatus down to the bone. You remove every layer between the file and the converter until what reaches the DAC is the bit-perfect master and *nothing else*. Then, and only then, when you have eliminated everything that is not the music, can you trust that the music is what you are hearing.

It is the same instinct that makes me build a compute cluster to test an idea rather than trust that the idea is right. Control the variables. Trust nothing you have not isolated. The audio chain is just a physics experiment where I happen to be the detector.

## The Software Pilgrimage

The hardware was settled. The software was a wilderness, and I wandered in it.

It started on a little Ryzen mini PC running Windows 11 Pro, with foobar2000[^12] — the old reliable — feeding a Moondrop Dawn Pro, a USB DAC barely bigger than the cable it dangles from. The Dawn Pro has a tiny indicator LED that changes color depending on what it is actually being fed: one color for CD-rate PCM, another for hi-res, another again for DSD. And with foobar handing the bits straight to the converter untouched, I could *watch* it work — the little light flicking from one color to the next as each track's true sample rate reached the DAC. It is a small thing. It is also a profoundly satisfying one, because it is the signal path *telling you the truth about itself*. The bits were getting through, and I could see them getting through. And it was never about the colour being an impressive one — a plain CD-rate file lighting its plain CD-rate colour was every bit as satisfying. It was about *honesty*: the little light could not lie to me about what was actually reaching the converter.

I tried Strawberry[^13] on Linux too, and leaned on Plexamp for the library, for the convenience of the whole thing in my pocket. I bounced between them, never quite satisfied — a resample here, a heavy interface there, a player that wanted to be a *platform* when all I wanted was a player. I landed, eventually, on fooyin[^14], once I had gone fully over to a Fedora Sway system: it talks to ALSA directly, it does not editorialize, it plays the file and gets out of the way, which turns out to be the only thing I have ever wanted from a music player and almost the only thing none of the others were willing to do.

And through this whole pilgrimage, one device disappointed me at every single turn: my MacBook Pro. An M3 Pro, eighteen gigabytes of memory, sold to professionals, priced for professionals, wrapped in the language of people who do serious work — and I could never once get that little Dawn Pro LED to change color on it. Whatever I plugged in, whatever I played, macOS sat itself down in the middle and handed the DAC its own resampled version, so the light just stayed one dumb, constant color — the audio equivalent of a closed door. A pro device with the pro features amputated. I noticed it as an annoyance. I filed it away as a quirk. I did not yet understand that it was a *symptom*, and that the disease it pointed to was much larger, and much worse, and aimed directly at me.

I will come back to it. Hold the thought.

## The Realization

This is the section where I was wrong about everything, found out, and had to rebuild what I believed from the foundation up. I have come to think of it as the most important thing audio ever taught me.

For a while I worshipped at the altar of high resolution. Twenty-four bits. A hundred and ninety-two kilohertz. The big numbers, the hi-res sticker, the conviction — never quite examined — that *more* meant *better*, that somewhere up there in the sampling rate was the fidelity I had been chasing since that dark theater. I spent real money on it. I felt the satisfaction of the large number, the way you do.

And then I sat down with Hilary Hahn playing Bach's Chaconne, in plain sixteen-bit, forty-four-point-one-kilohertz CD quality — the format everyone in my hobby had taught me to think of as the floor, the bare minimum, the thing you tolerate when you cannot get better.

And I understood — not as an argument, not as a fact I read, but in my *body*, the way I had understood the bass in that theater — that the number had never been the point.

CD quality is *enough*. It has always been enough. It was enough before I was born. The thing I had been hunting in those enormous sampling rates was simply not located in those enormous sampling rates. What I actually wanted, what I had always wanted, had a different name, and the name was **dynamic range** — the distance between the faintest breath of a sound and its loudest crash. The room a piece of music has to move in. The difference between a performance that *breathes* and one pressed flat against the inside of a pane of glass.

Let me be careful here, because this is the exact point where audiophiles curdle into zealots, and I refuse to. *Enough* is not the same as *contempt for more*. If an engineer cut a genuinely good high-resolution master, that is real craft, and I am not going to sneer at it because the number is bigger than I strictly need — that is just snobbery pointed the other way. And there is even an honest, physics-shaped reason a higher rate can be a shade better: every DAC oversamples before it reconstructs the analog wave, and a 44.1 kHz file forces a near-brick-wall reconstruction filter to thread itself between 20 and 22.05 kHz, smearing a little time-domain ringing right at the top edge of hearing. Feed the converter 96 or 192 and that filter gets room to breathe, its artifacts shoved up past anything human. So a higher rate is *better in a sense*. It is just not *necessary* — and it was never the thing standing between me and the music. The number was a distraction. The real problem was somewhere else entirely.

And here is the cruelty I had not seen, the thing that made me angry once I finally understood it: that breathing room is not taken from you by the *format*. A compact disc can hold enormous dynamic range. The reason so much modern music sounds crushed and airless and exhausting is not a technical limitation at all. It is a *choice*. It is the loudness war — decades of mastering turning everything up, compressing the life out of recordings, squeezing the quiet parts up toward the loud ones until there is nowhere left to go, because loud is what wins on the radio and in the store and in the thirty-second preview, and nuance is what loses. Greg Milner tells the whole long, sad history of it in *Perfecting Sound Forever*[^3], and reading it was like finally being handed the vocabulary for something I had been hearing my entire life without the words for. The medium was never the problem. The medium was *innocent*. The problem was that somewhere upstream, a person I would never meet had decided, on my behalf, that I did not need the quiet parts — and had taken them away before the music ever reached me.

And it is not only the mastering desk that reaches in to fill the quiet — it is also the player, at the end of every track, which is why I refuse crossfade. The silence between two songs is not dead air to be smoothed over; it is a rest, often one the artist sequenced on purpose, the half-second your ears need to set one thing down before they reach for the next. Fill it and you have decided, on the listener's behalf, that they cannot be trusted with a moment of quiet.

And I knew, without having to reason my way to it, exactly how much those quiet parts were worth — because there was already one recording that had taught me. A piece of music I had carried for a long time, that lives or dies on its silences, and that I have never once been able to listen to lightly.

## The Chaconne

This is the part I promised at the start — the one where I stop hiding behind the equipment. There is no graceful way into it, so I will just do it plainly, because plainness is the only thing that keeps it from turning into a performance.

There is a recording of the Chaconne — the final movement of Bach's Partita No. 2 in D minor, BWV 1004 — played by Hilary Hahn, from her 1997 Sony debut, *Hilary Hahn Plays Bach*. It is one violin. No accompaniment, no orchestra, no hiding place for the player. Seventeen minutes of a single human being and a single instrument and three hundred years of distance collapsing into nothing. It is the most important piece of music in my life. I am not being dramatic when I say that. I am being precise, in the way I try to be precise about everything, because precision is the only honesty I trust.

Most of the time, I listen to it the way I listen to everything now — closely, greedily, in plain CD quality through the chain I built for exactly this: the Mimir's clean, analytical resolution into the Midgaard on low gain, into the HD 650. And in sixteen bits and forty-four kilohertz, the format I had been taught to think of as the floor, it is *marvelous*. You can hear the bow bite into the string and drag across it, the faint grain of rosin under the hair, the breath of the bow change at the turn of a phrase, the sound of the room opening up around her in the half-second after a note lets go. Nothing is missing. Everything I was once told I needed a bigger number to hear is sitting right there, in the cheapest format that ever held music, and the HD 650 places it all somewhere just in front of me, coming from nowhere I can point to. That is the analytical pleasure of the thing, and it is real, and I love it.

But that is not why this is the most important piece of music in my life. That has to do with the nights.

There have been nights.

If you have had them, you already know the kind I mean, and there is nothing I can write that will tell you anything you do not already know in your body. And if you have *not* had them — if you have been spared — then I am genuinely, quietly glad for you, and I hope this stays an abstraction. But there have been nights where I could not bear to be inside my own head. Where everything was too loud and too sharp and too close, where the noise was not in the room but behind my eyes, and there was no volume knob for it, and no off switch, and nowhere to go, because the place I needed to leave was *myself*.

On those nights I would end up on the floor of my room. The room is about twelve square meters. There is a road bike clamped into its trainer, a piano, a sim rig, a desk — a whole life crammed into a space barely large enough to hold a person, let alone a person coming apart at the seams. But I managed. You find the space. You always find the space.

I would put the HD 650s on. And I want you to understand that the weight of them is *part of it* — the physical press of an over-ear headphone on your skull, the gentle clamp, the way they make you *stay*. They do not let you pretend you are doing something else. They are a small, deliberate act of being present, even when being present is the last thing you want.

And then I would start the Chaconne.

For the first bars I would still be shaking. The music does not fix you. I need to be clear about that, because the lie people tell about music is that it heals, and it does not heal, not like that, not on those nights. It does something quieter and more bearable than healing. Hahn begins, and there is that long, patient, unbearable descent — the theme unwinding, turning over itself, grave and slow and absolutely certain — and somewhere in there, somewhere I could never afterward locate, I would stop moving.

Not because I felt better. I want to be honest about that. Not because anything had been solved or lifted or made okay. But because, for seventeen minutes, the music was carrying the part of the weight I could not carry myself. It got underneath the worst of it and *held*, the way a strong arm gets under someone who is falling, and for as long as it played I did not have to hold all of it alone. That is the whole of it. That is what it did. It did not save me. It just stayed, and held its part, and would not let me be entirely alone with mine, until the worst of the wave had passed and I could breathe again.

And here is the part I find hardest to admit, because it quietly makes a joke of everything else in this post: for *this*, none of the gear matters. Not really. The Chaconne would do this to me through the thirteen-euro earphone. It did it to me long before I owned a single thing worth writing about. The amplifier, the balanced cables, the hours spent prying the Mimir loose from PipeWire so it could speak raw ALSA and *nothing* would touch the signal — all of it is real and I love all of it, and on a night like that it is almost entirely beside the point. A composer three centuries dead and a violinist I have never met reach across all that distance and keep me on the floor of my small room instead of somewhere worse, and they would manage it through a tin can and a length of string. The silences would still be there — those long rests Bach wrote in, where the sound falls away and leaves you holding your own breath — because the silence is not empty. The silence is not nothing. The silence is the part where you are allowed to breathe.

And then there is Ilaiyaraaja, who finds the same wound by the opposite road. With the Chaconne there are no words at all — it is pure instrument, and it reaches me with nothing in the way. With Ilaiyaraaja there are words, and I understand *every one of them*, because Tamil is my language and his music is the music of where I am from. There is no translating, no guessing at the shape of a feeling through phonetics — I get the whole thing, the melody and the meaning at once, in the tongue I was born into, and that complete understanding does not soften the blow. It lands harder. One reaches me because there is nothing to understand; the other reaches me because I understand all of it. Two roads, the same wound.

That is the center of it. Everything else in this post is true. That is the part that is *real*.

## The Refusal

Now I am angry, and I have earned the anger, and I want to be exact about who it is aimed at.

Go back to the MacBook. To the thought I told you to hold.

Apple sells hardware to professionals, prices it for professionals, drapes the whole thing in the language of serious work — and then quietly runs your audio through a layer that resamples whatever you feed it, downstream of any say you have in it. And here is the thing: I do not even need to argue that it sounds worse. Maybe you cannot hear it. That is not the point. The point is that it happens *in the dark*, on a machine sold to people who are supposed to care, with no light to tell you it is happening and no switch to turn it off. On Linux I can watch the exact bits land on the DAC and force the whole path bit-perfect if I want to — I am *allowed to know*. The Mac decides for you and will not even extend you the courtesy of saying so. A prosumer price with the one prosumer feature that actually matters — transparency — surgically removed. Make it visible. Give me the switch. That is the entire complaint.

And the moment you really see that pattern, you start seeing it *everywhere* — though I want to be precise, because these are not all the same crime. A lossy codec throws data away to save bandwidth. A hidden resample quietly changes the numbers downstream of you. The loudness war crushes the life out of a master back at the mixing desk — and that one is audible, and worse, it rides along inside the file no matter how you play it; a crushed CD and a crushed stream are equally crushed. Three different operations, three different mechanisms. What unites them is not the physics. It is that in every single case, someone upstream made the decision *for* you — about your fidelity, your dynamic range, your data — and built the machinery to enforce it whether you consented, or even knew.

Streaming is the worst of them, and Liz Pelly's *Mood Machine*[^4] is the autopsy. But I have to be honest about my own part in it, because it is not as clean as I would like. There are two rots here, and I only ever escaped one. The first is the *curation* rot — the algorithmic playlists, the Billboard-shaped queues, the lukewarm mood-slurry that exists to keep you mid-engagement and pacified. That one I genuinely dodged. For two and a half years I hunted by artist and chose every song; the conveyor belt slid past me untouched. But the second is the *surveillance* rot, and nobody dodges that one. Artist-first or not, every play I ever pressed was harvested, analysed, and folded back into the machine — the whole record of everything I had ever loved, turned into a product and sold back to me under the friendly name of *discovery*. I did not leave because of either of these. I left for the sound, in September, the way I already told you. It was only afterwards, reading Pelly, that I understood the second rot had been quietly feeding on me the entire time, immune as I had flattered myself I was.

There is a line in *Bohemian Rhapsody* I keep coming back to. Freddie Mercury, cutting a parasite out of his life, asks how you can tell when something has gone truly rotten — and answers himself: the fruit flies arrive, dirty little fruit flies, come to feast on what is left.[^7] That is the streaming economy in a single image. The model rotted music down into engagement-bait and a stream of data exhaust, and the rot is *exactly* what summoned the swarm — the playlist hustlers, the placement payola, the analytics machinery that turns your grief and your joy alike into a row in a database. I had thought that listening carefully kept me off the menu. It did not. The flies do not care how deliberately you choose your songs; they come for the rot, and the rot was never something any of us got to opt out of.

Glenn McDonald, who built Every Noise at Once and watched the genre machine work from the inside, writes about all of this in *You Have Not Yet Heard Your Favourite Song*[^5] with far more sympathy and patience than I can summon — and even his careful, generous version left me cold about where every line on the graph is pointing.

Here is the throughline, in case the slow build buried it: at every single layer — the format, the device, the platform, the master itself — there is a corporation standing between me and my own music, deciding what I am allowed to receive. The fidelity. The data. The taste. The silence between the notes.

I am done asking them nicely.

## The Work That Isn't Done

I am a physicist. When I do not trust a claim, I do not argue with it over drinks. I *measure* it. I build the apparatus that turns the claim into a number I can look at. So here is what I am building — all of it still in development, none of it finished, all of it pointed at the same target, all of it pieced together in whatever scraps of free time I can steal to sit down and work the next problem through.

**crête**[^9] is a zero-dependency C++17 dynamic-range analyzer. It computes the whole battery — TT-DR, EBU R128 integrated loudness, true peak, PLR, PSR, crest factor, the lot — and I have been grinding it, stubbornly, toward *bit-level* agreement with the reference tools the mastering world actually trusts. The numbers are not the point in themselves. The point is to take what loudness normalization and compression did to a master, in the dark, before it ever reached me, and drag it into the light where I can *see* it. To put the loudness war on a graph, so I never again have to sit there wondering whether a record was crushed or whether I imagined it. The realization from earlier in this post, turned into an instrument I can hold.

**tapedeck**[^10] is the other half of the answer, and it is aimed at the second rot — the surveillance one, the one that follows you out the door. Here is the trap: I left Spotify, but I still want to scrobble, to keep a running log of what I actually listen to. And every ordinary way of doing that — Last.fm, even ListenBrainz — means handing that log to someone else's server. So tapedeck is a self-hosted scrobbler, written in Rust, that keeps the record of my own listening *mine* — private, offline, on hardware I own, where no one can mine it, profile me from it, or ever sell it back to me as *discovery*. If Pelly's book is the diagnosis, tapedeck is me declining, with some finality, to be a data source for the disease — even after I thought I had already walked out of the building.

**AudioCheckr**[^11], my older artifact-detection tool, is parked. I am going to tear it down and rebuild the whole thing in C++ and do it *right* this time. I name it here because honesty demands the unfinished things get listed alongside the finished ones.

And then there is the one I genuinely cannot stop thinking about, the one that keeps me up.

Glenn McDonald's Every Noise at Once[^6] takes the entire genre-space of recorded music and maps it into a high-dimensional embedding — ten dimensions of *these sounds are near those sounds*. And I look at that and I do not see a map. A map is a flat, dead thing. I see a **manifold** — a curved, continuous space with its own intrinsic geometry — and the instant I see a manifold I want to do physics to it. What is the Riemann curvature tensor of music-space? Where does it bend, and around *what* — what gravitational mass of a genre warps the space near it? What does algebraic topology say about its *holes* — the sounds that, by the geometry, *ought* to exist in the gap between two others, and somehow do not? I have no idea whether any of it will mean a single thing. That uncertainty is precisely the reason to do it. And there is something I have been circling this whole essay without saying outright: the curvature I actually want to spend my life on is the curvature of *spacetime* — quantum gravity, the question of how mass bends the universe, the real physics I am training for. Yet here I am, itching to aim the very same instruments — the same Riemann tensor, the same topology — at the shape of music instead. As though the two were never separate questions. As though I had simply found the same problem twice, wearing different clothes. It is the part of me that sits on the floor and lets the Chaconne hold the weight, and the part of me that thinks in curvature and connected components, finally refusing to live in separate rooms.

The collection will keep growing past the point of all reason. The tools will keep being almost, never quite, finished. And somewhere in that twelve-square-meter room, on whatever night I next need it, the Chaconne will still be there — seventeen minutes long, full of silence, reaching across three centuries, holding its part of the weight so I do not have to hold all of mine alone.

That is music and me.

It was never really about the gear. The gear was only ever the apparatus. It was always, from that first dark theater to this last unfinished line of code, about not being alone with it.
